Corrective feedback loops

发表于2024-11-18|更新于2024-11-20|Thinking
|阅读量:

In an interview at the World Government Summit, Elon Musk was asked what his biggest challenge in life was. Elon took a few seconds to sort and sift through all the challenges and then answered:

“One of the biggest challenges I think is making sure you have a corrective feedback loop, and then maintaining that corrective feedback loop over time even when people want to tell you exactly what you want to hear…” — Elon Musk

So, what is the corrective feedback loops?

Corrective feedback refers to a common practice in the field of learning and achievement and usually involves students, teachers, employers and so on, who receiving formal or informal feedback from agents on their understanding or performance on various tasks, and if the correct corrective feedback is to be provided, then it must be non-evaluative, supportive, timely and specific(Wiki).

Who should provide corrective feedback?

If corrective feedback is directed at the target of advocacy, it should, wherever possible, be provided by people directly affected by the issue or by advocates who really know and understand the issue so that they can be a voice for those affected. It may be relatively easy to gather people in support of a cause, but unless they are very clear about what they are advocating for, they are unlikely to provide useful feedback and be listened to(University of Kansas).

When should you give corrective feedback?

Anytime is a good time to give corrective feedback, but in some cases it is especially appropriate.

When someone makes a request. Ideally an individual or organisation would ask for feedback on a regular basis or in specific situations. If the recipient is sincere in their request for feedback, they are more likely to take it seriously and act on it, rather than offering it out of the blue.

At the beginning of an advocacy campaign, especially at the planning stage. The quicker the feedback points out potential errors or inappropriate information, the less likely it is that the campaign will be compromised.

When the initial effects of the campaign can begin to be analysed, corrective feedback can help get the campaign back on track by identifying problems if some aspects of the campaign seem to be working poorly.

When the actions of the advocate or the target of the advocacy have had or are about to have unintended consequences. Unintended consequences are not always obvious. Corrective feedback can point out these consequences and help clarify what steps to take to prevent or eliminate them.

How do you give and accept corrective feedback?

Different kinds and sources of feedback attempt to convey messages to the receiver, but they vary in their emotional weight and degree of coercion (implicit or actual). An employer may provide an employee with what she perceives to be supportive feedback, but often - depending on their relationship and the character of the organisation - there is an implication hidden beneath the surface that if the employee does not mend his ways, he will lose his job. A consultant may be engaged to provide feedback to the organisation or to individuals within the organisation, but unless both the organisation and the individual are committed to taking the feedback seriously, the consultant may not bother at all.

Make your feedback formative, not summative. Formative feedback is designed to help the recipient improve his or her effectiveness. Summative feedback summarises the recipient and makes a judgement about their competence or personal worth. Therefore, providing formative feedback means:

  1. Feedback should focus on building skills and reinforcing areas for improvement rather than criticising or judging the recipient’s deficiencies.
  2. The provider should suggest some possible alternatives to what the recipient is doing.
  3. Feedback should help the recipient to set reasonable goals for changing and improving performance or behaviour.

Focus on specific problems without pointing fingers.

Identify the problem as clearly and specifically as possible. Once you’ve done that, keep exploring it. The question is not ‘Who is to blame?’ It’s ‘How can we make this work as smoothly as possible?’

Conclusion

As Elon Musk emphasises, corrective feedback loops are vital in all aspects of life. They involve an ongoing process of receiving, analysing and acting on feedback to ensure progress and improvement.

As for who should provide corrective feedback, it would be best if it were those directly affected by the matter or advocates with in-depth knowledge. Their insights are more valuable and relevant.

The timing of giving corrective feedback is very important. Whether it is in response to a request, in the early stages of an activity, when analysing initial effects, or in the event of unintended consequences, timely feedback can have a significant impact on the outcome of an action or project.

When giving and receiving corrective feedback, it is important that we understand the different types of feedback and their impact. It is vital that feedback is made formative rather than summative. This means focusing on skill building, suggesting alternatives and helping to set goals for improvement. In addition, concentrating on specific issues rather than blaming individuals creates a more productive environment for dealing with problems. In summary, understanding and effectively implementing corrective feedback loops can help make better decisions, improve performance, and adapt and grow in different jobs.
